# Why Don't People Vote in DAOs?

## The Plain English Version

### The problem

DAOs control billions of dollars, but typically only 2-8% of token holders bother to vote. That's like running a country where 95% of citizens stay home on election day. If almost nobody votes, a tiny group makes all the decisions -- which defeats the whole point of decentralization.

### What we did

We simulated thousands of DAOs with different voting rules and watched what happened to participation over time. We varied things like:
- How many votes you need for a decision to count (quorum)
- How often proposals come up
- How tired people get from voting repeatedly

### What we found

**There's a natural ceiling on participation, and you can't force your way past it.**

Every DAO has a "participation capacity" -- a maximum sustainable level of engagement. This is set by how many members you have, how much they care, and how quickly they get fatigued. Think of it like a bucket: you can pour in more water (more proposals, higher quorum), but the bucket only holds so much.

**Pushing harder makes things worse, not better.**

When we cranked up quorum requirements above 5%, proposals started failing -- not because people voted no, but because not enough people showed up. The DAO entered "governance gridlock" where nothing could get done.

**Voter fatigue is real and accumulates.**

Every time someone votes, they get a little more tired of voting. This "fatigue tax" builds up faster than it recovers. High-frequency voting burns out your most engaged members first, leaving governance in the hands of whoever hasn't quit yet.

### The bottom line

- Set quorum at about 80% of your natural turnout (for most DAOs, that means 3-5%)
- Start low and adjust up -- it's much easier than starting high and having governance break
- Let people delegate their votes to trusted representatives
- Don't schedule more votes than your community can sustain

**Real-world validation:** Compound and Uniswap both use ~4% quorum thresholds, which sits right in the sweet spot our simulations identified.
