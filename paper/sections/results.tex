\subsection{Baseline Characterization}

Before investigating specific research questions, we establish baseline governance dynamics. The academic baseline (Experiment~00, $N=30$ runs) produces a proposal pass rate of $98.5\% \pm 2.3\%$, average turnout of $22.7\% \pm 0.9\%$, and governance capture risk of $0.265 \pm 0.013$. Final treasury values average $\$11{,}597 \pm \$622$. These baseline values serve as reference points for all subsequent experiments.

\subsection{RQ1: Participation Dynamics}

\subsubsection{Calibration Effects (Experiment~01)}

Participation calibration targets significantly affect voter engagement. Across a $3 \times 3$ parameter grid ($N=270$ runs), we observe:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Turnout}: Ranges from $20.3\%$ to $22.6\%$ as voting activity targets increase, confirming that calibration parameters effectively modulate participation.
    \item \textbf{Pass rate}: Remains stable at $96.6$--$98.0\%$, indicating that participation changes do not destabilize governance outcomes.
    \item \textbf{Voter participation rate}: Reaches $25.9$--$27.4\%$, exceeding raw turnout due to delegation effects.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Quorum Sensitivity (Experiment~03)}

Quorum thresholds exhibit a non-linear relationship with governance outcomes. Sweeping quorum from $1\%$ to $50\%$ ($N=150$ runs):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pass rate}: Remarkably stable at $97.8$--$99.1\%$ across all quorum values, with a slight peak at $5\%$ quorum ($99.1\%$). Pass rate measures proposals that reach quorum, so stability reflects consistent voter support.
    \item \textbf{Quorum reach rate}: The critical metric---drops sharply from $100\%$ at $5\%$ quorum to $23.9\%$ at $20\%$ and $0\%$ at $40\%$+. Above $15\%$ quorum, most proposals expire without reaching quorum.
    \item \textbf{Turnout}: Stable at $22$--$23\%$ across all quorum settings, confirming that quorum thresholds do not themselves drive participation.
    \item \textbf{Critical threshold}: An inflection point in quorum reach appears at $15$--$20\%$, where the gap between required and actual participation causes governance gridlock.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{RQ2: Governance Capture Mitigation (Experiment~04)}

We test three capture mitigation strategies across a 24-configuration grid ($N=720$ runs): vote power caps, quadratic thresholds, and velocity penalties.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Vote power caps}: A cap of $10\%$ combined with a quadratic threshold of $250$ tokens reduces whale influence from a baseline of $0.454$ to $0.259$---a \textbf{$43\%$ reduction}.
    \item \textbf{Single entity control}: Decreases from $0.353$ to $0.271$ under the same cap configuration.
    \item \textbf{Governance capture risk}: Ranges from $0.267$ (with caps) to $0.465$ (without), with the strongest mitigations reducing risk by $43\%$.
    \item \textbf{Pass rate stability}: Remains at $97$--$99.9\%$ across all mitigation configurations, confirming that capture protections do not impair governance throughput.
    \item \textbf{Velocity penalties}: Show negligible effects on most metrics (Cohen's $d < 0.2$), suggesting that transaction-rate limits are less effective than structural voting reforms.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{RQ3: Proposal Pipeline Configuration (Experiment~05)}

Pipeline configurations (temp-check fractions, fast-track thresholds) are tested across $9$ configurations ($N=270$ runs).

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Temp-check effects}: Without temp-checks (fraction $= 0.05$), pass rates drop to $96.0\%$. At fraction $= 0.50$, pass rates reach $98.5\%$, confirming that preliminary screening improves governance quality.
    \item \textbf{Fast-track mechanisms}: Enabling fast-track with $12$-step minimum raises pass rates and maintains $99\%+$ quorum reach.
    \item \textbf{Proposal throughput}: $47$--$51$ total proposals per $720$-step simulation, with minimal variation across configurations.
    \item \textbf{Abandonment}: Zero proposals abandoned across all configurations, indicating that pipeline mechanisms redirect rather than discard proposals.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{RQ4: Treasury Resilience (Experiment~06)}

Treasury management parameters (buffer fractions, spending caps, stabilization) are tested across $12$ configurations ($N=360$ runs).

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stabilization impact}: Treasury volatility is $0.238 \pm 0.009$ with stabilization enabled versus $0.477 \pm 0.110$ without---a \textbf{$50\%$ reduction} in volatility.
    \item \textbf{Buffer effects}: Buffer fractions of $0.2$--$0.4$ combined with spending caps of $10\%$ yield final treasury values of $\$10{,}047$--$\$13{,}333$, with higher buffers providing greater protection at the cost of reduced capital deployment.
    \item \textbf{Governance capture interaction}: Capture risk drops from $0.450$ (no stabilization) to $0.266$ (stabilization enabled), suggesting that fiscal discipline reinforces governance integrity.
    \item \textbf{Growth tradeoff}: Treasury growth rate is $-0.736\% \pm 0.010$ (stabilized) versus $-0.687\% \pm 0.160$ (unstabilized), indicating a modest growth cost for substantially lower volatility.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{RQ5: Inter-DAO Cooperation (Experiment~07)}

Five cooperation topologies (homogeneous, heterogeneous, asymmetric, specialized, isolated) are tested ($N=150$ runs).

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Inter-DAO success rate}: $22.8$--$23.3\%$ across cooperative topologies, substantially above the isolated baseline of $0\%$ but reflecting the inherent difficulty of cross-organizational governance.
    \item \textbf{Collaboration rate}: $23.7$--$26.2\%$ of proposals involve cross-DAO elements.
    \item \textbf{Specialization advantage}: Specialized DAOs generate the most inter-DAO proposals ($74.3$ vs $47.6$ for homogeneous) and produce $48\%$ higher ecosystem treasury totals ($\$248$k vs $\$167$k).
    \item \textbf{Cross-DAO alignment}: Approval alignment ranges from $0.286$ to $0.296$, with specialization providing the highest alignment.
    \item \textbf{Individual DAO pass rates}: Range from $70.5\%$ to $87.5\%$, with heterogeneous governance configurations performing best.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Supporting Experiments}

\subsubsection{Scale Effects (Experiment~08)}

Scaling member count from $50$ to $500$ ($N=150$ runs) reveals:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pass rate}: Increases from $92.5\%$ ($n=50$) to $99.8\%$ ($n=500$), indicating strong positive scaling effects.
    \item \textbf{Participation rate}: Decreases from $26.4\%$ ($n=50$) to $21.4\%$ ($n=500$), consistent with participation dilution at scale.
    \item \textbf{Capture risk}: Decreases from $0.299$ ($n=50$) to $0.254$ ($n=500$)---a $15\%$ reduction, suggesting that scale itself provides partial capture resistance.
\end{itemize}
