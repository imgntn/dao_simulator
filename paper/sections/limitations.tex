\subsection{Limitations}

\subsubsection{Model Simplifications}

Our agent models make several simplifying assumptions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Static preferences}: Agents don't learn or adapt over time
    \item \textbf{No social dynamics}: No influence, persuasion, or social pressure
    \item \textbf{Limited adversarial modeling}: Attack/defense scenarios in experiment~07 only; no general strategic manipulation
    \item \textbf{Isolated system}: No external market or protocol interactions
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Mechanism Coverage}

While we implement several voting mechanisms, we omit:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Futarchy (prediction market governance)
    \item Holographic consensus (boosted proposals)
    \item Optimistic governance (veto-based systems)
    \item Multi-sig and council-based governance
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Validation Constraints}

External validity is limited by:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Lack of ground-truth agent behavior data
    \item Difficulty isolating mechanism effects in real DAOs
    \item Rapidly evolving DAO landscape
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Calibration Uncertainty}

Our calibration targets derive from observational data on existing DAOs, which introduces several sources of uncertainty:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Selection bias}: Observable DAOs may differ systematically from failed or inactive DAOs not captured in available datasets
    \item \textbf{Temporal variation}: DAO behavior varies with market conditions; our calibration reflects 2022--2024 averages and may not generalize to other periods
    \item \textbf{Measurement error}: On-chain metrics may not capture off-chain coordination, informal governance discussions, or signaling votes
    \item \textbf{Parameter sensitivity}: While we conducted sensitivity analyses, some parameter interactions may remain unexplored
\end{itemize}

Future work should incorporate Bayesian calibration methods to quantify parameter uncertainty and propagate it through simulation outputs, providing credible intervals rather than point estimates.

\subsubsection{Parameter Interaction Effects}

Our experiments varied parameters independently (one-at-a-time sensitivity analysis). However, governance mechanisms may interact in complex, non-linear ways:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Quorum thresholds may interact with delegation rates to produce threshold effects
    \item Vote power caps may interact with proposal frequency to affect throughput
    \item Treasury buffer sizes may interact with attack intensity in non-monotonic ways
\end{itemize}

Full factorial designs or response surface methodology would require prohibitively many simulation runs but represent an important direction for future research with sufficient computational resources.

\subsubsection{Computational Constraints}

Our analysis is bounded by:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Grid sweeps explore pairwise interactions; higher-order interaction effects remain unexplored
    \item Configurable simulation length (default 2{,}000 steps)
    \item Limited agent heterogeneity configurations
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future Work}

\subsubsection{Near-Term Extensions}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{LLM-based agents}: Use language models for more realistic agent reasoning
    \item \textbf{Multi-parameter sweeps}: Grid and random search over parameter space
    \item \textbf{Adversarial agents}: Model strategic behavior and attacks
    \item \textbf{Network effects}: Add social network structure
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Medium-Term Goals}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Blockchain validation}: Calibrate against on-chain data from real DAOs
    \item \textbf{Mechanism synthesis}: Use evolutionary algorithms to discover optimal mechanisms
    \item \textbf{Real-time analysis}: Integrate with live DAO data for monitoring
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Long-Term Vision}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Governance-as-a-Service}: Provide simulation-backed recommendations to DAOs
    \item \textbf{Formal verification}: Prove properties about mechanism designs
    \item \textbf{Cross-DAO analysis}: Study inter-DAO coordination and competition
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Open Questions}

This work raises questions for future research:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item What is the optimal trade-off curve between decentralization and efficiency?
    \item How can DAOs maintain engagement at scale without professionalizing governance?
    \item What mechanisms are robust to both apathy and adversarial behavior?
    \item How should DAOs evolve their governance as they mature?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Ethical Considerations}

Governance simulation tools could in principle be used to identify and exploit governance vulnerabilities. We mitigate this risk by: (1) focusing on defensive analysis and mechanism robustness rather than attack optimization; (2) releasing the framework as open-source to enable community scrutiny; and (3) framing results as conditional hypotheses rather than actionable exploits. We encourage responsible use of simulation tools for governance improvement rather than manipulation.
