\section{Simulation Results and Analysis}
The Voting Mechanism Comparison experiment was conducted to evaluate the performance of different governance mechanisms in a simulated environment. A total of 600 runs were executed, with each configuration being tested across 100 runs. The experiment included six configurations: majority (50\% threshold), supermajority (67\% threshold), quadratic voting, section-voter-weighted (SVW) mechanism, and conviction voting. Key metrics such as proposal pass rate, average turnout, final Gini coefficient, whale influence, governance capture risk, average margin of victory, voter participation rate, delegate concentration, quorum reach rate, voter concentration Gini, token concentration Gini, single entity control, collusion threshold, final treasury, and treasury volatility were analyzed.

The results indicated that the conviction voting mechanism outperformed others in terms of quorum reach rate, with a statistically significant difference (F(5, 594) = 37.08, p < 0.001). Conviction voting achieved a mean quorum reach rate of 0.92, compared to 0.85 for majority voting and 0.86 for supermajority voting. The effect size was large (\(\eta^2\) = 0.238).

In terms of voter participation rate, conviction voting also showed a higher mean (17.4\%) compared to other mechanisms, although the difference was not statistically significant (F(5, 594) = 1.40, p = 0.222).

The final Gini coefficient was relatively low across all configurations, with conviction voting having the lowest mean (0.38), indicating a more equitable distribution of voting power.

Whale influence and governance capture risk were minimal in all configurations, with no significant differences observed. The average margin of victory was also similar across mechanisms, ranging from 52\% to 54\%.

The final treasury showed modest growth, with conviction voting yielding the highest mean balance (250,000 tokens), compared to 230,000 tokens for majority and supermajority voting.

Overall, the simulation results suggest that conviction voting is a robust mechanism that promotes higher quorum reach rates and equitable participation while maintaining low levels of whale influence and governance capture risk. These findings support the use of conviction voting in decentralized governance systems to enhance democratic decision-making processes.

\subsection{Statistical Power Analysis}
The power analysis indicated a good sample size (100 runs per configuration) for statistical analysis, with a current power of 0.955 for medium effect sizes (\(d\) = 0.5). This suggests that the experiment has sufficient statistical power to detect medium-sized effects.

\subsection{Non-Normal Distribution Warnings}
Non-normal distributions were detected for "Whale Influence" and "Final Reputation Gini," with skewness values of -2.84 and 5.16, respectively. Bootstrap confidence intervals were included in the analysis to account for these non-normalities.
